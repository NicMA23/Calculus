Limits approaching infinity

$$\lim _{x\to \:0}\left(\frac{1}{\left(x\right)^2}\right) = 0$$
$$\lim \:_{x\to \:0^+\:\:}\frac{1}{x^2}=\left(\frac{1}{\left(0.000...1\right)^2}\right)=+\infty$$
$$\lim \:_{x\to \:0^-\:\:}\frac{1}{x^2}=\left(\frac{1}{\left(-0.000...1\right)^2}\right)=+\infty$$
As the left-hand limit of zero is equal to the right-hand limit of zero,
the limit of x approaching zero exists and is infinite.

When we consider the limit of x approaching 0, it means we are
examining the behavior of x as it gets arbitrarily close to 0.
Even if the function or expression is not defined at x = 0, the limit
can still exist and have a finite or infinite value. In this case, as x
approaches 0, the limit exists and tends to infinity
