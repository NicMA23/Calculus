Limits approaching a constant

$$\lim _{x\to \infty \:}\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0$$
$$\lim _{x\to \infty }\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\:=\:\left(\frac{1}{\infty \:}\right)\:=\:0$$ As the numerator approaches infinity, the result tends to zero.
As the result tends zero, this tells us that the horizontal asymptote is at zero
However, horizontal asymptotes are not always present, for example:

$$\lim _{x\to \infty \:}\frac{x^2}{2x}\:=\:\lim \:_{x\to \:\infty \:\:}\frac{x}{2}\:=\:\frac{\infty }{2}\:=\infty$$

Dominant Term Theorem

$$\lim _{x\to \infty }\left(\frac{x^6+3x^5-90x^2}{x^7-\frac{7}{4}x^4}\right)\:=\:\lim \:_{x\to \:\infty \:}\frac{x^6}{x^7}=\lim \:\:_{x\to \:\:\infty \:\:}\frac{1}{x}\:=\:0$$ To find the limit of x approaching infinity for a division of polynomials, follow these three steps:

  1. Identify the dominant terms: Determine the terms with the highest degree in the numerator and
    denominator. These terms will have the greatest impact as x approaches infinity.

  2. Simplify the dominant terms: If possible, simplify or cancel out common factors in the dominant terms to
    make the expression more manageable.

  3. Substitute x, if present, with infinity: Replace any occurrence of x in the expression with infinity to
    evaluate the limit as x tends to infinity.
